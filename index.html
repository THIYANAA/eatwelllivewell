The Five
Food Groups

Every food group plays a unique role in keeping your body strong, energised and healthy. Adolescents need serves from all five every single day.

🌾
Grain Foods
Grain foods are edible seeds from grasses. They contain essential energy, fibre, and key nutrients like B vitamins, iron, and magnesium. Grain foods also reduce risks of heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and stroke. Choose wholegrain varieties where possible for maximum nutritional benefit.
B VitaminsIronMagnesiumFibreEnergy
7–8
serves per day
e.g. 1 slice bread
½ cup cooked rice
🍎
Fruit
Fruits are the mature, seed-bearing part of a flowering plant. Fruit contains vitamins C, A, and folate, potassium, magnesium, water, and fibre. It also boosts immune function, improves digestion, and lowers the risk of chronic diseases like heart disease, stroke, and cancer.
Vitamin CVitamin AFolatePotassiumFibre
2
serves per day

½ cup berries
🥦
Vegetables
A vegetable is any edible part of a plant, such as roots, tubers, stems, leaves, or flower buds. Vegetables are rich in vitamins A, C, and K, and folate. They also reduce the risk of heart disease, stroke, chronic illnesses, and certain cancers.
Vitamin AVitamin CVitamin KFolateAntioxidants
5–6
serves per day
e.g. ½ cup cooked veg
1 cup salad leaves
🥛
Dairy & Alternatives
Dairy foods are from the milk of mammals, mainly cows, but also sheep, goats, and buffalo. Dairy gives your body calcium, protein, and vitamin D. These nutrients help your body have strong bones, help your muscles recover and support immune function.

Meat & Protein Foods
Meat foods are from animals, like hens, cows, pigs and more. Meat gives your body vitamin B12, protein, iron and zinc. These nutrients help your body with muscle growth and repair, energy, prevent iron deficiency and give you a strong immune system.

      Healthier

      See how simple swaps can turn everyday recipes into more nutritious, balanced meals — without losing the taste!

      🍚 Fried Rice
      Swapping white rice for brown and adding more vegetables makes this classic much more nutritious.

      🔴 Original Recipe

      Classic Fried Rice

      • White rice
      • 2 eggs
      • Soy sauce
      • Frozen peas
      • Oil
      • Optional ham
      Method
      1. Heat oil.
      2. Add eggs and scramble them.
      3. Add rice, peas, soy sauce, and ham.
      4. Stir-fry until it is hot.

      🟢 Modified Recipe

      Healthier Fried Rice

      • Brown rice MOD
        White rice
      • 1 egg + 1 extra egg white MOD
        2 eggs
      • Low-sodium soy sauce MOD
        Soy sauce
      • 1 cup mixed vegetables (corn, peas, carrot) MOD
      • ½ cup tofu cubes MOD
      • 1 tsp oil (reduced) MOD
      Method
      1. Heat a small amount of oil.
      2. Add egg and egg white.
      3. Add rice, vegetables, tofu, and soy sauce.
      4. Stir-fry until warm.
      🥢 Vegetable Spring Rolls
      Baking instead of deep frying significantly reduces fat while keeping them crispy.

      🔴 Original Recipe

      Vegetable Spring Rolls (Fried)

      • Spring roll wrappers
      • 1 cup shredded cabbage
      • 1 cup grated carrot
      • ½ cup bean sprouts
      • 1 tbsp soy sauce
      • Oil for deep frying
      Method
      1. Mix the vegetables and soy sauce.
      2. Wrap the mixture in spring roll wrappers.
      3. Deep fry until golden.
      4. Serve with dipping sauce.

      🟢 Modified Recipe

      Baked Vegetable Spring Rolls

      • Spring roll wrappers
      • 1 cup shredded cabbage
      • 1 cup grated carrot
      • ½ cup bean sprouts
      • ½ cup mushrooms (added) MOD
      • 1 tbsp low-sodium soy sauce MOD
        1 tbsp soy sauce
      • 1 tsp sesame oil (added) MOD
      • Spray oil (instead of deep frying) MOD
        Oil for deep frying
      Method
      1. Mix vegetables, mushrooms, soy sauce, and sesame oil.
      2. Wrap mixture in spring roll wrappers.
      3. Bake at 200°C for 15–20 minutes.
      4. Serve with a small amount of dipping sauce.
      🍢 Chicken Teriyaki Skewers
      Leaner chicken, added vegetables and air-frying make this a much healthier option.

      🔴 Original Recipe

      Classic Chicken Teriyaki Skewers

      • 500g chicken thigh, cut into cubes
      • ¼ cup soy sauce
      • ¼ cup brown sugar
      • 1 tbsp sesame oil
      • 1 tbsp honey
      • 1 garlic clove, crushed
      • 1 tsp grated ginger
      • Wooden skewers
      Method
      1. Mix soy sauce, brown sugar, sesame oil, honey, garlic, and ginger.
      2. Add the chicken and marinate for 30 minutes.
      3. Thread chicken onto skewers.
      4. Grill or pan-fry for 10–12 minutes until cooked.
      5. Serve warm.

      🟢 Modified Recipe

      Healthier Chicken Teriyaki Skewers

      • 500g chicken breast (leaner) MOD
        500g chicken thigh
      • 2 cups vegetables — capsicum, zucchini, onion (added) MOD
      • ¼ cup low-sodium soy sauce MOD
        ¼ cup soy sauce
      • 1 tbsp honey only (no brown sugar) MOD
        ¼ cup brown sugar + 1 tbsp honey
      • 1 tsp sesame oil (reduced) MOD
        1 tbsp sesame oil
      • 1 garlic clove, crushed
      • 1 tsp grated ginger
      • 1 tbsp lemon juice (added) MOD
      • Wooden skewers
      Method
      1. Mix low-sodium soy sauce, honey, sesame oil, garlic, ginger, and lemon juice.
      2. Add the chicken and vegetables and marinate for 20–30 minutes.
      3. Thread chicken and vegetables onto skewers.
      4. Bake or air-fry at 180°C for 12–15 minutes until cooked.
      5. Serve while warm.

      Dietary Guidelines
      The 6 Key Guidelines

      Evidence-based recommendations from the National Health and Medical Research Council (NHMRC).

      01

      Eat from all five food groups every day

      Include plenty of vegetables, fruit, grain foods, lean meats/alternatives, and dairy every day. Variety ensures you get all the essential vitamins and minerals your body needs.

      02

      Enjoy water as your main drink

      Teens should aim for 8–10 glasses of water daily. Avoid sugary drinks like soft drinks and energy drinks — they contribute to tooth decay and excess kilojoule intake.

      03

      Limit saturated fat, added salt & sugar

      Highly processed snack foods and fast food should be occasional treats, not everyday foods. Excessive saturated fat and sugar is linked to obesity and cardiovascular disease.

      04

      Encourage breastfeeding (for carers)

      This guideline applies to infant nutrition but builds the foundation of healthy eating habits from birth — setting up lifelong healthy patterns.

      05

      Prepare and store food safely

      Safe food handling prevents foodborne illness. Always refrigerate perishables, wash hands before eating, and use clean utensils — especially important for packed lunchboxes!

      06

      Achieve and maintain a healthy weight

      Balance kilojoule intake with at least 60 minutes of physical activity per day. This supports both physical and mental wellbeing throughout adolescence.

      Protein
      45–65g per day
      Essential for muscle growth, tissue repair, and producing enzymes and hormones. Especially important during the rapid growth of adolescence.

      Water
      2–2.6 litres per day
      Regulates body temperature, transports nutrients, lubricates joints, and aids digestion. Dehydration reduces concentration significantly.
